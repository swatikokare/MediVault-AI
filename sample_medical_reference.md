# Sample Medical Reference For RAG Testing

## 1. Migraine

Migraine is a recurrent headache disorder often described as moderate to severe throbbing pain, usually on one side of the head. It may be associated with nausea, vomiting, sensitivity to light, sensitivity to sound, and worsening with physical activity. Some patients experience aura, such as flashing lights, blind spots, tingling, or temporary speech difficulty before the headache begins.

Common triggers include poor sleep, skipped meals, dehydration, stress, alcohol, menstrual hormonal changes, strong smells, and some foods. A headache diary can help identify patterns.

Initial self-care may include resting in a quiet dark room, drinking fluids, applying a cold compress, and taking clinician-approved pain relief early in the attack. Some patients are prescribed triptans or preventive medicines when attacks are frequent or disabling.

Urgent medical care is needed for a sudden worst-ever headache, headache after head injury, headache with fever and neck stiffness, new weakness, confusion, seizure, fainting, vision loss, or a new severe headache during pregnancy.

## 2. Appendicitis

Appendicitis is inflammation of the appendix. Pain often begins near the navel and then moves to the lower right side of the abdomen. Common symptoms include loss of appetite, nausea, vomiting, low-grade fever, abdominal tenderness, and pain that worsens with movement, coughing, or walking.

Appendicitis is usually treated as a medical emergency because the appendix can rupture. Treatment commonly involves surgical removal of the appendix, called appendectomy. Antibiotics may be given before surgery and may be used in selected uncomplicated cases under medical supervision.

A person with suspected appendicitis should not delay care, should avoid eating or drinking until evaluated, and should avoid taking laxatives or applying heat to the abdomen.

Urgent medical evaluation is needed for worsening right lower abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, fever, rigid abdomen, fainting, or signs of shock.

## 3. Type 2 Diabetes

Type 2 diabetes is a chronic metabolic condition in which the body has difficulty using insulin effectively. Symptoms may include increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurred vision, slow wound healing, recurrent infections, and unexplained weight changes. Some people have no symptoms and are diagnosed through blood tests.

Management usually includes nutrition planning, regular physical activity, weight management when appropriate, blood glucose monitoring, and medicines such as metformin or other clinician-prescribed therapies. Good foot care, eye examinations, kidney monitoring, and blood pressure control are important parts of long-term care.

Warning signs needing urgent care include confusion, severe dehydration, repeated vomiting, very high blood glucose with illness, chest pain, shortness of breath, or signs of severe low blood sugar such as sweating, shaking, confusion, seizure, or loss of consciousness.

## 4. Asthma

Asthma is a chronic airway condition that can cause wheezing, coughing, chest tightness, and shortness of breath. Symptoms may worsen at night, during exercise, with respiratory infections, or after exposure to triggers such as dust, smoke, pollen, cold air, or animal dander.

Asthma treatment often includes a quick-relief inhaler for sudden symptoms and a controller medicine for people with frequent symptoms or repeated flare-ups. Correct inhaler technique is important. Patients may be asked to follow an asthma action plan that explains daily treatment and what to do when symptoms worsen.

Emergency care is needed if breathing is very difficult, lips or face look blue, the person cannot speak full sentences, symptoms do not improve after using a rescue inhaler as directed, or drowsiness/confusion occurs.

## 5. Hypertension

Hypertension means persistently elevated blood pressure. It often has no symptoms, which is why regular measurement is important. Long-term uncontrolled hypertension increases the risk of stroke, heart disease, kidney disease, and vision problems.

Management may include reducing sodium intake, regular physical activity, limiting alcohol, stopping tobacco use, weight management when appropriate, stress reduction, and medicines prescribed by a clinician. Home blood pressure logs can help track control.

Urgent medical care is needed for very high blood pressure with chest pain, shortness of breath, severe headache, confusion, weakness on one side, vision changes, or difficulty speaking.

## 6. Medication Safety

Patients should tell clinicians about all prescription medicines, over-the-counter medicines, supplements, allergies, pregnancy status, kidney disease, liver disease, and previous adverse drug reactions. Medicines should be taken only as directed. Doses should not be doubled after a missed dose unless a clinician or medication label specifically says to do so.

Urgent help is needed for signs of a severe allergic reaction, such as swelling of the face or throat, wheezing, trouble breathing, widespread rash, dizziness, or collapse.
